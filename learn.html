Overflowing landfills are absolute reminder of our lack of responsibility and discipline. Too much garbage harms our planet and its inhabitants, but there is a direct solution, the proper waste segregation. Waste segregation helps to reduce the amount of waste that we release into the environment.

What is Waste Segregation?

Waste Segregation is a process of sorting and categorizing garbage into different types, such as biodegradable, recyclable, and residual at the point of disposal. This simple method keeps recyclables out of landfills while reducing environmental and health risks.

Why it matters?

We have been continuously warn about rising waste production and highlight the importance of proper waste management. Waste comes in several forms and must be handled differently. Therefore, understanding waste segregation and how to segregate garbage are critical to meeting overall waste management goals.

Benefits of proper waste segregation

Segregating your waste has many benefits. It contributes to environmental protection, support better health, and keeps your place and community safe. Recycling materials can help you save money on disposal while possibly makes you money.

Segregating your trash properly helps ecosystem by:

1. Environmental protection

  • Reducing pollution.
  • Enabling recycling saves natural resources and reduces the amount of energy and raw materials required to manufacture new things.
  • Reducing waste that ends up in landfills.
  • Food waste in landfills generates methane, a potent greenhouse gas.

2. Health protection

  • Preventing toxic chemicals and elements in hazardous waste from ending up in landfills and harming water and soil.
  • Safe storage helps to prevent the spread of hazardous microorganisms and reduces pests.

3. Economic benefits

  • Reducing disposal costs by reducing the amount of waste delivered to landfills, and thereby lowering associated taxes and fees.
  • Additional earnings from sellable recyclables like paper, metals, and plastics.

Types of Waste

• Biodegradable

Organic materials that decompose naturally that includes vegetable and fruit peels, food leftovers, and yard waste.

Note: Can be used as back-garden compost for household plants.

• Non-Biodegradable/ Recyclable

Non-organic materials that cannot decompose naturally. Materials that can be recycled and used to make new product that includes polymers, metals, glass, and synthetic materials.

Note: Clean and dry them thoroughly before throwing them into the recycling bin.

• Residual/ E-waste

Materials that can endanger human health or the environment, such as batteries, chemicals, pesticides, electronic waste such as computers, smartphones, printers, and other electronic appliances.

Note: Keep hazardous chemicals or electronics separated safely from general household trash.

Quick Tips:

  1. Create a three-bin system - You don’t need fancy bins or labeled cartons, old buckets will do.
  2. Rinse before you bin - Quickly rinse plastic containers and cans so they don’t smell or attract pests.
  3. Compost if possible - Set up a small backyard compost area for fruit and vegetable peels to provide rich soil for plants.
